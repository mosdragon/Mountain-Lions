CombiningConcepts

What Defines a Collision?

Collisions are simply when two bodies strike each other. The two types of collisions are inelastic and elestic. In this case, our collision will be the latter. Elastic collisions can best be thought of as car crashes. Car crashes generally have two vehicles crashing into one another, but neither car gets stuck to the other. Keeping this in mind with the law of Conservation of Energy, we can fully understand that a car will experience all sorts of forces on it, and thus the passengers will experience these forces as well due to inertia. Crash test dummies are used to evaluate how safe or dangerous a vehicle is under certain conditions. Generally, vehicles are designed with a front end that will crumple up, so as to reduce the force the passengers in the vehicle experience during a collision.

COLLISIONS



Observe the golf ball in the video above. See how it collided violently into the steel plate while traveling at 150 mph? Saw how its shape got eskewed momentarily as all of the momentum ceased and the object began traveling in the opposite direction? It crumpled up a bit to have less of an impulse on the remaining portion of the ball. Impulses are the truly lethal component of car crashes. Too much impulse can cause a violent jerk in the joints and bones or even cause internal bleeding. An impulse is simply a force experienced over a period of time. By creating vehicles that crumple easily, car companies help reduce the impulses felt by the passengers inside simply because the ends that crumple delay the time and weaken the force of the crash. In regards to motion, the inertia acting on the passengers is weaker when the whole car has more time to slow its movement. In regards to energy, a vehicle has plentiful kinetic energy before the collision, energy that will transform into other types of energy during the collision. With a front end that crumples, some of the kinetic energy is transformed and used towards crumpling the front ends, leaving less kinetic energy to be transformed into potential, which would cause painful impulses on the passengers inside the vehicles. Finally, in regards to focees, colliding vehicles will exert forces upon one another. If the momentum for one vehicle is greater than for the other, the end result after the collision will be both vehicles moving slowly in the direction of the original velocity vector for the vehicle with the greater momentum.
